Understanding Semantle

Semantle is a daily word puzzle that uses word2vec technology to measure the semantic similarity between words. Unlike Wordle, which tests your knowledge of letter positions, Semantle tests your understanding of word meanings and associations. When you guess a word, the game gives you a similarity score from -100 to 100 (with 100 being an exact match), indicating how closely related your guess is to the target word in meaning. The Semantle daily puzzle resets every 24 hours, offering a fresh word to discover each day.

Learning how to play Semantle requires a fundamentally different approach than most word games. There are no letter clues — only numerical similarity scores. You need to think about what words are semantically related to the target and use the scores to navigate toward the answer. Semantle rules allow unlimited guesses, but finding the exact word can take dozens or even hundreds of attempts. The game rewards players with a strong sense of word associations and the patience to methodically explore semantic neighborhoods.

Tips for Improving Your Semantle Scores

Start with diverse category words. A proven Semantle strategy is to begin with words from different semantic categories — emotion words, nature words, action words, abstract concepts. This scatter-shot approach helps you find a high-scoring neighborhood quickly, which you can then explore more systematically.

Explore semantic neighborhoods thoroughly. Semantle tips from experienced players emphasize that once you find a word with a decent similarity score, you should explore that area intensively. Think about synonyms, related concepts, and words that appear in similar contexts. The target word is likely within the same semantic cluster as your best guess.

Think about word context, not spelling. Learning how to get better at Semantle involves shifting your thinking from letter-based word games to meaning-based associations. Words that share no letters can be semantically very close (like "hot" and "warm"), while words that share many letters can be semantically distant (like "warm" and "worm"). Focus on meaning and usage, not spelling, and you'll find the target word much more efficiently.
